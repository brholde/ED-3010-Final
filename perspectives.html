Perspectives

One of the major selling points of monitoring software such as GoGuardian and LanSchool is that they help monitor what students are searching and typing on their devices. The purpose of this is to promote safety. When a student searches for keywords that indicate they are intending to engage in self harm, violence against other students, or other dangerous activities, it is flagged and sent to school officials. This helps to stop dangerous situations before they even start.

There are very limited studies indicating how effective the software is at keeping students safe, there are documented instances where the software has had successful outcomes. In 2019, Lois Beckett wrote an article for the Guardian and provided a few examples of monitoring software preventing tragedy. In one scenario, a school official received an email in the middle of the night informing them that a student was writing about self harm. The official was able to call the authorities to get the student help. The student was given mental health treatments in a hospital. Another account discusses a student searching how to kill themself. When the authorities arrived, the student’s mother claimed the student was asleep. (Beckett, 2019). In some scenarios, the software is more aware of a student’s mental health than teachers and parents.

Those in support of the software take the stance that the software is needed for student safety despite the potential breach of student privacy. With this stance, the potential to help a student undergoing a mental health episode outweighs the negative outcomes associated with student surveillance.

While the potential benefits are enticing with these types of monitoring software, the negatives need to be considered as well. Because these softwares are designed to help catch potential threats or student mental health crises, the software is very intrusive by nature.

One concern is how monitoring software tends to run all day, everyday despite the student’s location. In a 2025 study, 86% of the monitoring services studied conducted 24/7 monitoring on the student (O'Daffer et al., 2025). Even when a student is making searches or working on an assignment at their own home, they are being logged. For many, this can feel scary and uncomfortable. Furthermore, students do not get a say on whether or not their school device is monitored. It is preinstalled and unremovable.

An arguably bigger issue with monitoring software is that it collects massive amounts of data on students and can be easily abused. Those that are given advanced privileges in the software such as teachers and administrators have the potential to view what a student is doing on their device at all times and the student would be none the wiser. This can be a scary thought, especially for parents. Additionally companies such as GoGuardian claim that the student data that they collect is unidentifiable and impossible to trace back to the student. However, this is not necessarily true. With advanced data processing techniques, data can possibly be reattributed back to the student (Kelley, 2023).

Those that take the privacy stance believe that at a minimum, the way that monitoring software is used needs to be dialed back. Examples of how this could be done include limiting to only monitor student devices during school hours, giving better information to students on what is being monitored, and ensuring any dangerous material flagged by the software is first reviewed by a human before action is taken.
